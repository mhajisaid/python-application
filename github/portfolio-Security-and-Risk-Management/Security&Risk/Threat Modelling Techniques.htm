Threat Modelling Techniques

 

 

Threat modelling is a systematic approach which involves the identification and assessment of possible security threats to a system (Shostack, 2014, p.9). Threat modelling techniques are essential as they can help in the identification of potential security vulnerabilities during the initial stages of software or system development prior to being exposed to any cyberattacks (Tuma et al., 2018). Thus, this could possibly result in enhanced security, a reduction in the risk of data breaches, and increased trust in the system. Within this context, the following section of the report will explore two widely used threat modelling techniques, namely STRIDE and attack tree, as well as demonstrating how they can be used to identify potential cyber threats to an online shopping system alongside appropriate solutions to mitigate these threats.

 

STRIDE


STRIDE is a widely adopted threat modelling framework that identifies six primary categories of threats, including Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service, and Elevation of Privilege (Hussain et al., 2014). In the context of an online shopping system, STRIDE can be used to analyse potential threats to the system by focusing on the different categories of threats and the potential risks linked to them. As explained by Azam et al., (2022), it is essential to design a system not only with threat modelling techniques in mind but to incorporate a GDPR compliant approach throughout the design process to ensure trust from the initial stages of the project.

Spoofing is the act of assuming the identity of another entity, individual, or system to deceive or mislead a user (Shostack, 2014, p. 64). Cybercriminals may attempt to impersonate users by using false credentials to gain access to sensitive information or carry out unauthorized actions (Azam et al. 2022). For example, looking at the use case diagram presented earlier, an attacker may try to gain access to an existing customers account which would give them further unauthorised access to the payment the card payment services or third part payment services of the customer, since they are linked to the account. As explained by Shostack (2014, p. 146), this risk can be reduced by implementing thorough authentication measures such as multi-factor authentication.

Moreover, tampering is a threat in which unauthorised modification of data takes place, typically by an attacker who is not authorised to make such changes. (Khan, 2017). In this instance, the attacker could gain access to the server of the online shopping system and make changes to the data which is held. To mitigate this and maintain integrity, as suggested by Khan (2017) the system should verify that the data received from any user has not been altered or tampered with. As opposed to this method, Shostack (2014, p. 16) explains that repudiation is a threat where attackers deny actions which have been performed on the system, such as altering their account information or making a purchase. To address this, comprehensive logging and audit trails must be implemented to track user actions and prevent any repudiation attempts (Shostack, 2014, p. 16). This could possibly be approached by monitoring the pattern of the way customers use the system and flagging abnormal behaviour in contrast to the typical usage patterns presented in the activity diagram above.

Similarly, information disclosure, denial of service and elevation of privilege are further methods attackers could use to deny access and cause disruption to the system (Shostack, 2014, p. 64). A possible solution to these issues is to implement adequate access controls and monitor privileges. By restricting the listed users on the case diagram to access to only the resources they require for their specific tasks, the likelihood of unauthorised access or actions can be reduced.

 

 

Attack Tree

The attack tree below also shows some possible ways an attacker could exploit vulnerabilities and reach their goal in a particular system. This attack tree displays various stages of an attack, where each node represents a particular step in the attack process (Dewri et al, 2012). The child nodes branching out from the parent nodes demonstrate different paths or specific tactics that the attacker may take (Dewri et al, 2012). By using the attack tree technique, potential threats to the system can be identified and evaluated in terms of their significance.

One of the key advantages of the attack tree technique Is that it provides a structured visualisation of potential attack scenarios, and clearly outlines the prioritisation of threats. This approach enables us to assess the weaknesses in the system's design alongside the use case and activity diagrams; revealing how activities and actions can be compromised and exploited.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

However, as noted by Nagaraju et al. (2017), one of the key limitations of the attack tree method is that it does not illustrate a full picture of all the potential threats to the system. It might be challenging to foresee every situation and attack, particularly when dealing with highly elaborate attacks.

In conclusion, threat modelling and the use of techniques such as STRIDE and attack trees are essential in identifying and mitigating potential threats to a system or application such as the online shopping system. Through the use of these techniques, potential security risks can be identified and addressed early in the development process, leading to a more secure and reliable system. However, it is important to note that these techniques should not be used in isolation, and a comprehensive security approach involving multiple layers of protection is necessary to ensure the safety and security of the system and its users.
