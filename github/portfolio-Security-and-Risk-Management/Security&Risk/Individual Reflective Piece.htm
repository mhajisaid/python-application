Individual Reflective Piece

 

The Security and Risk Management module enabled me to gain a greater understanding of the relationship between security and risk in todays technology-led business world. This included being introduced to the concept of Industry 4.0 which is quickly changing the way in which businesses operate and leading the shift towards digitalisation (Kovaitė and Stankeviciuses, 2019). Moreover, the initial units of the modules also allowed me to gain a greater understanding of the risks that are part of digitalisation as well as enabling me to explore the risk management process and the varying methodologies involved in the process. For instance, the effects of different assessment types, such as qualitative and quantitative methods were reviewed, alongside reviewing the role of user participation in the process.

These theoretical aspects of the module were particularly useful in the first assessment The Development Team: Risk Identification Report. This enabled me to further my understanding of risk and risk management approaches by applying it practically. As part of my team, I had the responsibility of carrying out the risk assessment of Pampered Pets and the existing operations of the business. This required the selection of a risk assessment methodology, for which a qualitative approach was chosen, which allowed me to identify risks and prioritise different areas of concern. The threat modelling techniques from the third and fourth unit, STRIDE and DREAD, were also explored to examine possible threats and categorise the identified risks.

Researching risk management approaches and justifying this approach significantly broadened my understanding of how different risks assessment methodologies and the theoretical knowledge gained in the initial units could be employed in real-world digitalisation scenarios. For example, it was important that the business needs were taken into consideration and the chosen approach was tailored to manage the concerns this particular business may face during the digitalisation process.

Figure 1: A Gantt Chart outlining the timeline for the Risk Identification Report.

Moreover, the initial risk identification group assessment, gave me the opportunity to take part in a simulated scenario in which I played the role of a member of a professional development team. The responsibility of breaking down the risk assessment into actionable steps and outlining the plan for my team was an important task. The practical aspects of producing a comprehensive Gantt chart (as shown above outlining a project plan, keeping project records, taking detailed meeting notes, and drafting a project introduction provided an insight into the collaborative and organisational skills that are required in a professional setting, particularly with geographical and time constraints involved. Similarly, leading the planning sessions and structuring the risk assessment task highlighted the significance of a well-organised approach in the risk management process.

The second half of the module introduced concepts of quantitative risk modelling which were very intriguing and new to me. This covered methods like Monte Carlo simulations and Bayes theorem-based techniques, which involve probabilities, along with multi-criteria decision analysis methods like TOPSIS, AHP, and ANP. I was particularly interested in risk matrices; this included understanding the components involved in risk such as the severity of a threat and probability (Olson and Deshen, 2020, p.9). As a student with a mathematical and logical way of thinking, I was intrigued by this approach towards having a greater understanding of the impact of risk by quantifying the potential loss as well as the severity of threat. Wider reading into these concepts and pursuing this interest was particularly useful for the second group assignment in which these tools could be practically applied, especially in evaluating the potential risks following the digital transformation of the business. Thus, applying quantitative risk modelling in Unit 8 was useful in preparation for the second group project, as I was able to create a QR model of a real-world situation. This exercise allowed me to assess my understanding of the new concepts and assess the effectiveness of this approach.

Similarly, the concepts of Business Impact Assessments, Recovery Time Objectives, Recovery Point Objectives and Disaster Recovery covered in the unit 9 and 10 were very useful in understanding some of the concerns and security risks associated with digitalisation and the impact on the supply chain. Gaining further knowledge of these concepts was beneficial and I took the initiative to ensure that our team maintained a coherent approach towards addressing the challenges posed by the new scenario in the second assignment. The quantitative risk assessment, as part of the project, was a meticulous process that required a thorough understanding of the probabilistic methods involved. It was an opportunity to practically apply the quantitative risk modelling techniques explored within the module; I was responsible for outlining the approach, using these methods to calculate the risk, and presenting the results and recommendations for the business. Upon reflection, this was a great opportunity of practising the evaluation process of the real-life risks involved in business.

Overall, I believe that my professional development has been greatly improved by the Security and Risk Management module. In terms of skills I have gained for my career, it has strengthened my fundamental understanding of risk management and cybersecurity; having now gained experience in both qualitative and quantitative risk assessments. The practical aspects of this module, particularly the group projects, improved my communication, leadership and teamwork skills which has prepared me for facing challenges and working as part of a team in the cybersecurity industry. Personally, the module enabled me to recognise my particular interest in quantitative risk management which I hope to explore and pursue further.
