Technical Risks frequently materialize in the form of System Compatibility problems. A prominent illustration is the case of Boeing's 737 Max, which confronted significant technical compatibility issues. These issues arose due to the inadequacies in the integration of a new software system, known as the Maneuvering Characteristics Augmentation System (MCAS), with the existing flight control systems. This incompatibility played a role in tragic accidents and amplified apprehensions regarding the technical risks linked to intricate system integrations.

 

Data Security Risks are widespread in the modern digital landscape. An illustrative real-world instance is the Equifax data breach, where insufficient security measures enabled a cybercriminal to capitalize on a vulnerability, jeopardizing the confidential information of countless consumers. Likewise, Technical Risks emerge from the delegation of cloud computing management services, as exemplified by the Capital One data breach. In this case, a technical lapse involving a misconfigured web application firewall laid bare extensive personal data, underscoring the critical need for safeguarding sensitive information.

 

Unit 2- Differences between Qualitative and Quantitative assessments

 

Qualitative and quantitative risk assessments differ in their approach, data type, accuracy, and complexity.

Qualitative assessments rely on subjective, descriptive evaluations of risk, often using terms like high, medium, and low. They depend on expert opinions and are valuable for initial risk identification and communication but lack precision and objective data. In contrast, quantitative assessments use numeric data and mathematical models to quantify risks in terms of probabilities, financial impact, or other measurable units. They provide more precise results, making them suitable for in-depth analysis and decision-making.

 

Quantitative assessments require expertise in data analysis and modelling, while qualitative assessments are accessible to a broader audience. The former offers greater accuracy due to empirical data, whereas the latter is quicker and easier to perform. A combination of both approaches is often used, with qualitative assessments serving for quick risk identification and communication, while quantitative assessments provide a detailed understanding of the potential impact, helping in prioritizing risks and resource allocation.

Unit 4- how to select the most appropriate threat modelling tool for various situations

 

To choose the most appropriate threat modelling tool:

 

Define Objectives: Clearly outline your goals for threat modelling, whether it's vulnerability identification, risk prioritization, or security education.

Assess System Complexity: Consider the complexity of the system you're analysing. Choose a tool that aligns with the system's intricacy.

Resource Compatibility: Ensure the tool fits your available resources, whether you need a cost-effective open-source solution or a more comprehensive commercial tool.

Compliance Alignment: Check if the tool supports regulatory or industry standards your organization must comply with.

Trial and Feedback: Trial the tool in a real-world scenario, gather feedback, and assess how well it integrates into your workflow.

Unit9: Lecture notes: Explain what is meant by BC, DR, RTO and RPO

 

BC (Business Continuity): Business continuity refers to an organization's ability to maintain essential functions, processes, and services during and after a disaster or significant disruption. The goal of business continuity planning is to ensure that core business operations can continue with minimal or no interruption.

 

DR (Disaster Recovery): Disaster recovery is a subset of business continuity that focuses on the recovery and restoration of IT systems, data, and technology infrastructure following a disaster or outage. It involves strategies and procedures to recover IT resources and resume operations as quickly as possible.

 

RTO (Recovery Time Objective): RTO is the maximum allowable downtime for a specific business process, application, or system. It represents the time frame within which an organization aims to recover and restore functionality after a disruption. For example, if a system has an RTO of 4 hours, it must be back up and running within 4 hours of a disaster.

 

RPO (Recovery Point Objective): RPO is the maximum tolerable data loss an organization can afford following a disruption. It represents the point in time to which data must be recovered to resume normal operations without significant impact. For instance, if an application has an RPO of 1 hour, data loss must be limited to no more than 1 hour's worth of data.

Unit12: Describe the main emerging trends in the field

 

Zero Trust Architecture (ZTA) is a cybersecurity approach that challenges the traditional trust model of "trust but verify." In ZTA, trust is never assumed, and all users, devices, and network traffic are treated as untrusted, even if they are within the corporate network. ZTA is based on the principle of continuous verification and strict access controls. It emphasizes identity and context-based access, requiring strong authentication, authorization, and ongoing monitoring of user and device behaviour. ZTA aims to minimize the attack surface, enhance security, and protect against insider and outsider threats by reducing trust in any entity and verifying each access request, regardless of its origin, making it a fundamental concept in modern cybersecurity.
